Why Animal Breeding Matters

Animal breeding has transformed livestock productivity over the past century:

  • Dairy cattle produce 4-5√ó more milk than their ancestors from the 1950s
  • Broiler chickens reach market weight in half the time with dramatically improved feed efficiency
  • Swine produce larger litters with leaner, faster-growing offspring
  • Beef cattle exhibit improved carcass quality, feed efficiency, and maternal ability

These improvements directly impact:

  • üåç Food security: Efficient animal protein production for a growing population
  • ‚ôªÔ∏è Sustainability: Reduced environmental footprint per unit of product
  • üí∞ Economic viability: Improved profitability for livestock producers
  • üêÑ Animal welfare: Selection for health, longevity, and reduced stress

Understanding animal breeding is essential for careers in production agriculture, genetics companies, breed associations, and agricultural research.

What You‚Äôll Learn: Chapter-by-Chapter Overview

Part 1: Foundations of Animal Breeding

Chapter 1: Introduction to Animal Breeding

Learn how the animal breeding industry works:

  • What is animal breeding and why does it matter?
  • How genetics companies generate revenue (semen sales, females, embryos, genomic testing)
  • Industry structure by species (swine, poultry, dairy, beef, horses)
  • Major players: Genus/PIC, Cobb-Vantress, Select Sires, breed associations
  • Pyramid breeding structures (nucleus-multiplier-commercial)

Key takeaway: Understand the business context that drives breeding decisions.

Chapter 2: Selection Basics

Master the fundamentals of genetic selection:

  • Traits of economic importance (production, reproduction, health, efficiency, quality)
  • Phenotyping: how we measure traits accurately
  • Data recording and quality control
  • Defining breeding objectives
  • Introduction to True Breeding Value (TBV)

Key takeaway: High-quality data is the foundation of genetic improvement.

Chapter 3: Basic Genetic Model

Learn to partition phenotype into genetic and environmental components:

  • The basic model: y = Œº + A + E
  • Additive vs. dominance vs. epistatic effects
  • Why additive effects drive selection response
  • Variance partitioning (œÉ¬≤_P = œÉ¬≤_A + œÉ¬≤_D + œÉ¬≤_E)
  • Parent-offspring relationships

Key takeaway: Only additive genetic effects are reliably transmitted to offspring.

Chapter 4: Quantitative vs. Simply Inherited Traits

Understand different types of genetic architecture:

  • Simply inherited traits (single genes, major effects): coat color, polled/horned, genetic defects
  • Quantitative traits (many genes, small effects): growth, reproduction, production
  • Why most economic traits are quantitative
  • Threshold traits and mixed models
  • Modern genomics bridges the gap

Key takeaway: Breeding strategies differ for simply inherited vs. quantitative traits.

Part 2: Genetic Parameters

Chapter 5: Heritability and Repeatability

Learn the most important parameter in animal breeding:

  • Heritability (h¬≤): proportion of variance due to additive genetics
  • Interpreting heritability (high: >0.40; moderate: 0.20-0.40; low: <0.20)
  • Common misconceptions about heritability
  • Repeatability (r): upper limit of heritability
  • Permanent environmental effects (PE)

Examples: Growth traits (h¬≤ = 0.35), reproductive traits (h¬≤ = 0.10), carcass traits (h¬≤ = 0.45)

Key takeaway: Higher heritability means faster response to selection.

  • The equation: R = (i √ó r √ó œÉ_A) / L
  • Selection intensity (i): how hard we select
  • Accuracy (r): how well we predict breeding values
  • Genetic standard deviation (œÉ_A): amount of genetic variation
  • Generation interval (L): age of parents when offspring are born
  • Trade-offs among the four factors (especially accuracy vs. generation interval)
  • Comparing selection strategies (mass selection, progeny testing, genomic selection)

Key takeaway: Genomic selection resolves the accuracy vs. generation interval trade-off.

Part 3: Selection Methods

Chapter 7: Estimating Breeding Values

Learn how to predict genetic merit:

  • Simple methods: own performance, pedigree index, progeny average
  • Contemporary groups: accounting for environmental differences
  • BLUP (Best Linear Unbiased Prediction) - the modern standard
  • Understanding EBVs (Estimated Breeding Values)
  • Accuracy and reliability of predictions
  • Examples: Dairy Net Merit, Beef EPDs, Swine EBVs

Key takeaway: BLUP uses all information optimally to estimate breeding values.

Chapter 8: Genetic Correlations and Correlated Response

Understand how traits are genetically linked:

  • Genetic correlation (r_A): correlation between breeding values for two traits
  • Causes: pleiotropy (same genes) or linkage (genes on same chromosome)
  • Interpreting correlations: positive, negative (antagonistic), zero
  • Correlated response to selection
  • Important examples:
    • Dairy: milk yield vs. fertility (r_A ‚âà -0.35) ‚ùå
    • Swine: growth rate vs. backfat (r_A ‚âà +0.30) ‚ùå
    • Beef: weaning weight vs. yearling weight (r_A ‚âà +0.70) ‚úÖ

Key takeaway: Negative genetic correlations require multi-trait selection.

Chapter 9: Multiple Trait Selection and Selection Index

Learn to balance improvement across multiple traits:

  • The need for multi-trait selection
  • Breeding goal (H) vs. selection index (I)
  • Economic weights: how much is each trait worth?
  • Selection index theory: b = P‚Åª¬π G v
  • Calculating and interpreting index weights
  • Real-world examples: Dairy Net Merit, Beef Terminal Index, Swine Sow Index

Key takeaway: Selection indices optimally combine traits based on economic value and genetic parameters.

Part 4: Mating Systems and Crossbreeding

Chapter 10: Mating Strategies and Optimum Contribution Selection

Learn to make smart mating decisions:

  • Selection vs. mating decisions
  • Types of mating systems (random, assortative, compensatory)
  • Managing inbreeding and genetic diversity
  • Inbreeding coefficient (F) and inbreeding depression
  • Optimum Contribution Selection (OCS): maximize gain while constraining inbreeding
  • Software tools: MateSel, AlphaMate

Key takeaway: Mating decisions affect both genetic gain and long-term genetic diversity.

Chapter 11: Crossbreeding and Hybrid Vigor

Exploit heterosis for improved performance:

  • What is crossbreeding and why crossbreed?
  • Heterosis (hybrid vigor): superior performance of crossbreds
  • Genetic basis: dominance, overdominance, epistasis
  • Traits with high heterosis: reproductive traits (5-20%)
  • Crossbreeding systems: terminal cross, rotational, composite breeds
  • Industry structure:
    • Swine: (Large White √ó Landrace) F‚ÇÅ √ó Duroc terminal sire
    • Beef: Crossbred cows √ó terminal sire
    • Poultry: Proprietary four-way crosses

Key takeaway: Crossbreeding is nearly universal in commercial swine and poultry production.

Part 5: Genomic Tools

Chapter 12: Introduction to Genomics

Learn about DNA markers and genomic technologies:

  • What is genomics in animal breeding?
  • SNPs (Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms): the most common genetic markers
  • SNP chips (arrays): genotyping thousands to millions of SNPs
  • Bioinformatics basics: quality control, imputation
  • Whole genome sequencing (WGS)
  • Applications: genomic selection, parentage verification, GWAS, inbreeding management

Key takeaway: Genomic technologies have revolutionized animal breeding since ~2008.

Chapter 13: Genomic Selection

Understand modern genomic prediction methods:

  • What is genomic selection? Predicting breeding values from SNP data
  • Why it works: linkage disequilibrium (LD) between SNPs and causal mutations
  • Genomic BLUP (GBLUP): using genomic relationship matrix (G)
  • SNP-BLUP: estimating effects of individual SNPs
  • Bayesian methods: BayesA, BayesB, BayesC (allow SNPs to have different effects)
  • Single-step genomic BLUP (ssGBLUP): the industry standard
  • Impact: doubled rate of genetic gain in dairy cattle

Key takeaway: Genomic selection enables high-accuracy predictions for young animals without waiting for progeny.

Part 6: Practical Applications

Chapter 14: Practical Animal Breeding and Data Skills

Build practical skills for industry careers:

  • SQL basics for querying breeding databases
  • APIs for accessing genomic data and services
  • Data management and quality control
  • Animal identification systems (RFID, DNA-based parentage)
  • Phenotypic culling vs. genetic selection
  • Career paths: geneticist, data manager, reproductive technician, bioinformatician
  • Software used in industry: BLUPF90, ASReml, MateSel

Key takeaway: Successful breeders need both theoretical knowledge and practical data skills.
